Found in translation / 001 (Maybe) Clouds

This piece is a sky. The question behind it was simple: what makes a cloud a cloud? Before any code, it was worth looking closely. A cloud is never the same twice. It's soft, with no real edges, it just thins out into air. It has detail at every scale at once, big masses, smaller billows, then fine wisps. And it's uneven, dense in one place, torn open in the next, lit on one side and shadowed on the other.

Each of those traits became a rule. One layer decides where there is cloud at all and where there's open sky, fading softly at the edges instead of cutting off. Another adds the inner texture, pushed and pulled so it swirls as if moved by wind. A slower one decides how much texture shows, so some regions stay calm and others turn turbulent. Stacked together, they give each point a density: how much cloud is here.

Only at the end does that density become a color. The same structure can be a clear blue afternoon or a glowing dusk, depending on how it's read, the clouds staying white against blue, or catching warm light on their lit edges while their undersides hold the shade. The shape and the light are decided separately, so one sky can be carried through every hour of a day without moving a single cloud.

Creative Coding?

Creative coding is making images by writing rules instead of drawing. There's no canvas in the usual sense and no brush. There's a program, and the program decides what every pixel becomes.

The way it works is almost stubbornly simple. An image is just a grid of points, each one knowing only where it sits. The program looks at each point in turn and answers a single question: given this position, what color should you be? Asked across millions of points, those individual answers add up to an image, none of it placed by hand.

What makes this interesting is where the control sits. You don't shape the result directly. You shape the rules that produce it, then let them run, and a lot of what appears you didn't explicitly design, it followed from the rules in ways you don't fully see coming. Randomness usually plays a part, so the same program can produce something different every time. Working this way feels less like drawing and more like setting up conditions and seeing what grows out of them.
